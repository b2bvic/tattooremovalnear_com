## Treatment Process and Pain Management

Consultations assess Fitzpatrick skin type, ink depth, and expectations. **Wood's lamp** examination detects metallic pigments.

**Q-switched lasers** emit 5-10 nanosecond pulses at 1064nm for black, 532nm for reds. Sessions last 5-25 minutes. **Picosecond lasers** deliver 450-750 picosecond pulses creating photoacoustic shockwaves.

Pain management includes topical **lidocaine 5%** cream and **Zimmer Cryo** cooling. **Park Nicollet** offers **Pro-Nox** nitrous oxide at $100-$150 additional. Injectable lidocaine available for large tattoos, adding $125-$225.

Aftercare involves antibiotic ointment and dressings for 72 hours. Avoid sun for 4 weeks, no pools for 7-10 days.

## Minnesota Regulations and Licensing

**Minnesota Statutes § 147.081** requires laser operators to work under physician supervision or hold APRN licenses with laser training. The **Minnesota Board of Medical Practice** mandates 40 hours education and 20 supervised procedures.

Facilities register devices with **Minnesota Department of Health** and maintain liability insurance. Check credentials through Minnesota medical board databases.

## Session Frequency and Timeline

Minneapolis practitioners space treatments 8-12 weeks apart. Black ink clears in 6-8 sessions over 12-18 months. Resistant colors require 10-14 treatments spanning 20-28 months.

The **Kirby-Desai Scale** predicts counts using skin type, location, colors, density, scarring, and layering. Progress plateaus may require wavelength switches or extended 12-16 week intervals.

## Minnesota Climate Considerations

Minnesota's extreme winters (December-February average 5-25°F) require special planning. Indoor heating dehydrates skin—use humidifiers and moisturize 3x daily. Snow and ice increase fall risks during vulnerable healing (7-10 days post-treatment).

Summer's moderate sun (UV index 7-9) demands photoprotection 4 weeks post-treatment. **Lake activities** (10,000+ lakes) require caution. Wait 2 weeks before swimming in lakes, which harbor bacteria.

Spring and fall offer optimal treatment windows with temperatures (40-65°F) and manageable sun exposure.
